Most cleaning marketplaces treat your home like a Uber ride.

Fastest, cheapest, no memory of who you are. Everclean is the opposite — same staff each visit, your allergies and access codes saved (encrypted), a subscription model so the value compounds month over month. It's a service the way a good hotel concierge runs one.

Vertical Next.js 14 platform, not a marketplace.

Prisma 5.22 over PostgreSQL with role-based auth (NextAuth v5, four roles). Stripe Subscriptions API with tiered discounts. Upstash Redis pipeline for atomic rate limiting (5 distinct policies). Sentry across all three Next.js runtimes. 200 m geofence validation on staff GPS check-ins. Auto-generated bookings on subscription renewal.

02 · The bet

Why I picked this fight.

Four short answers to the four questions every project should answer before the first commit.

Signal

The middle was empty.

Cleaning marketplaces compete on price; local boutiques compete on service but lose on convenience. The HNW segment is squeezed between commodity and friction. The middle is where the bet sits.

Filter

Vertical, subscription, premium, local-depth-first.

Own the staff bench. Subscription tiers reward continuity. Local depth before geographic expansion (the playbook ports — same template + new staff bench).

Why now

The window is open and tightening.

Suburban affluent regions are under-served by tech-native premium services. HNW now expects digital convenience but won't accept marketplace commoditization. Larger players will figure out the same gap eventually.

Why me

I have an alarm code I won't give a marketplace.

The privacy-of-access-codes problem was personal before it was technical. I value the same person knowing my home. I built what I'd actually pay for.

03 · No-jargon explainer

Problem. Solution. Result.

01 · Problem

Marketplaces optimize for cheapest, not best.

Existing apps are commodity races to the bottom: cheapest provider, shortest visit, no memory between bookings. Privacy is an afterthought; data sells; staff turnover is invisible to the client until something breaks. Luxury homeowners pay for continuity — and continuity is precisely what marketplaces structurally cannot offer.

02 · Solution

Build a vertical, not a marketplace.

One brand, one staff bench, one client portal. Subscriptions instead of one-shot bookings. GPS-validated check-in/out so the schedule matches reality. Encrypted client preferences (alarm codes, lockbox combos, allergies) so the second visit is better than the first. Tier discounts that reward continuity. Quiet Luxury design language so the app looks like the service it promises.

04 · My process

How I shipped this without a team.

Four habits, applied recursively. The same discipline that kept the platform feeling luxury kept the build cheap.

01 · The editorial bar

Would Aman Resorts ship this?

If the feature would feel cheap to a luxury hotel guest, it gets cut. The bar is unforgiving — it eliminated the "progress badge that gamifies cleaning" idea, the "AI-powered smart suggestions" upsell, the dark patterns in the cancel flow. What survived is what would survive a concierge's handoff between staff. That's the design test.

02 · Scoped agents per concern

Design system, schema, auth+billing, infra — four sessions.

Cross-cutting touch only at the boundaries: design tokens are JSON, the schema is Prisma, the auth contract is the NextAuth session type. Each agent reads the boundary doc, not the whole project. The infrastructure session never sees component-level UI; the design session never sees rate-limit code.

Tokens are the schema.

Design tokens (brand.primary.500, etc.) reused across 28 routes — referencing them by name is dramatically cheaper than re-prompting. Same for schema: Prisma types flow through Zod flow through React Hook Form — one source, three consumers, no re-explanation.

06 · Stakeholder lens

Five perspectives I held at the same time.

A solo builder is also their own product manager, business person, ops lead, legal scout, and growth marketer. Each lens left fingerprints on the design.

Customer (HNW)

I want the same person knowing my house. I won't give my alarm code to a marketplace. I'll pay 30% above market if I never have to re-explain the dog, the espresso machine, the orchids. Continuity is the feature; everything else is the price.

Business

LTV math: 1 client × 12 mo × $2,400 avg = $28,800/yr lifetime. 50 clients = $1.4M ARR; 200 = $5.7M. Tier discounts (15/10/5%) trade margin for retention. Cost-of-goods is the staff bench, not the platform.

Operations

GPS check-in validates labor-cost claims. The 200 m geofence is liability protection — if the cleaner wasn't on-site when the property was damaged, the audit trail says so. Staff substitution requires client pre-approval — retention dressed as policy.

Legal & Privacy

GDPR + privacy of homes. Alarm codes, lockbox combos, smart-lock instructions encrypted at rest, decrypted only for assigned staff on an active booking. Photos auto-delete at 30 days, GPS at 90.

Growth

$100 referral reward (subsidized from LTV math). Gift cards as gateway purchase — recipient becomes a subscription candidate. Regional expansion playbook: each city is the same template + a new staff bench.

08 · Engineering proof

One artefact. One editorial decision.

Including this exhibit because the interesting part isn't the syntax — it's the editorial decision that "fail open + log loudly" beats "fail closed + lose the lead" for a luxury brand. Different category, different choice.

EXHIBIT · DECISION · FAIL OPEN, LOG LOUDLY

When Redis goes down, the contact form should still work.

Rate limits exist to protect the system, not to brick the contact form on the day Upstash has an outage. V2.0's rate-limiter wraps every Redis call in a try/catch — when the pipeline fails, the limiter returns success: true, degraded: true and Sentry records the incident. Better to admit one extra request than to lose a customer because we got loud about being safe.

V1.1 · in-memory 
const hits = new Map<string, number>();
export function rateLimit(key: string) {
  const n = (hits.get(key) ?? 0) + 1;
  hits.set(key, n);
  return n <= 100;
  // resets on every redeploy.
  // useless on Vercel.
}
V2.0 · graceful degradation 
import { Ratelimit } from
  '@upstash/ratelimit';
import { redis } from '@/lib/redis';
import { captureException } from
  '@sentry/nextjs';

export const rateLimits = {
  general: new Ratelimit({ redis,
    limiter: Ratelimit.slidingWindow(100, '1 m') }),
  strict:  new Ratelimit({ redis,
    limiter: Ratelimit.slidingWindow(20,  '1 m') }),
  auth:    new Ratelimit({ redis,
    limiter: Ratelimit.slidingWindow(5,   '15 m') }),
  booking: new Ratelimit({ redis,
    limiter: Ratelimit.slidingWindow(5,   '1 m') }),
  admin:   new Ratelimit({ redis,
    limiter: Ratelimit.slidingWindow(200, '1 m') }),
};

export async function check(
  policy: keyof typeof rateLimits,
  key: string,
) {
  try {
    return await rateLimits[policy].limit(key);
  } catch (err) {
    // fail open. log loudly.
    captureException(err, {
      tags: { policy, key },
      level: 'warning',
    });
    return { success: true, degraded: true };
  }
}
09 · Deep dive

The 200 metre rule, visualised.

A check-in inside the gold ring records. A check-in outside is rejected and counted toward a failure-tracking lockout.

The math

Haversine on the sphere — closed-form, one division, no iterative solve. Earth radius treated as 6,371 km. Within ~0.5% accuracy at the city scale.

// great-circle distance between two lat/lng points
a = sin²(Δφ/2) + cos(φ₁)·cos(φ₂)·sin²(Δλ/2)
d = 2·R·asin(√a)

// rule: reject if d > 200 m
accepted = d ≤ GEOFENCE_M

11 · Decisions

Five questions worth answering.

Strategic first, technical last. The technical decisions are downstream of the strategic ones.

01
Why this market — and why Waterloo Region specifically?
+
HNW homeowners in suburban affluent regions are under-served by tech-native premium services.
The HNW segment is squeezed between marketplace commoditization (TaskRabbit, Handy, Tidy) and premium-but-friction local boutiques. Waterloo Region was the right starting point because it's affluent enough to support the price point ($2,400 avg/year), small enough to dominate, and adjacent to a tech ecosystem primed for digital convenience. The playbook ports — each city is the same template plus a new staff bench. The platform is the moat.
02
Why a vertical platform instead of a marketplace?
+
Continuity. Marketplaces optimize for cheapest provider; luxury optimizes for the same person on the second visit.
A marketplace would be cheaper to build and easier to scale, but it would solve a different problem. Luxury homeowners pay for memory: the staff who knows the alarm code, the espresso machine, the dog's name. Encoding that requires owning the staff bench, the schedule, and the client preferences as one connected system.
03
Why subscriptions instead of one-shot bookings?
+
The relationship needs a shape. Tier discounts trade margin for retention. LTV math justifies it.
One-shot bookings are a marketplace mechanic. The continuity bet requires a recurring relationship — and a subscription with continuity-rewarding discount tiers is the simplest way to encode that. The LTV math works: a 15% weekly discount paid against a 12-month retention at $2,400/yr is a profitable trade.
04
Why audit-grade infrastructure in V2.0 before scaling clients?
+
The category is luxury. The first incident in front of a client is also the last client.
A Stripe webhook that drops a customer.subscription.deleted event and silently re-bills the client is a credibility event, not a bug. A staff time-clock that doesn't validate GPS is a liability event. Sentry across three runtimes catches what intuition won't. Upstash rate-limits + graceful degradation keep the contact form working on Upstash's bad days.
05
Why NextAuth v5 (beta) instead of waiting for stable?
+
App Router support, Edge runtime support, type-safe session — the v4-to-v5 gap was wider than the beta-stability gap.
NextAuth v4 predates the App Router and middleware-based auth. V5's auth() helper works in Server Components, route handlers, and middleware uniformly. The session is typed end-to-end so auth.user.role is 'CUSTOMER' | 'ADMIN' | 'MANAGER' | 'STAFF' everywhere.

13 · Reflection

What surprised me. What I'd change. What's next.

What surprised me

The schema grew bigger than the visible product.

Eight new models in V2.0 is bigger than the original V1.1 schema. The continuity bet (subscriptions, GPS, gifts, referrals, notifications) is structurally larger than the booking surface that visibly carries it. The visible product is the tip; the schema is the iceberg.

What I'd do differently

Validate the LTV math before building the tier system.

Ship V1.1 to 5 real clients before building the V2.0 infrastructure. Validate the LTV math against real bookings before building the tier-discount system that depends on it. The infrastructure was correct; the order would have de-risked it.

What's next

UI for what V2.0 plumbed. Then the second city.

V2.0-complete user-facing UI. Maybe pricing-tier optimization once real LTV data exists. Regional expansion playbook documented — the second city is the proof; the third is the pattern.
